Introduction

  1. The section Using the EIST later in this guide has instructions for creating an environmental indicators summary (EIS).
  2. Learn the science behind the EIST after this introduction.
  3. At the end of this guide, reference the Glossary of Terms and Acronyms and look for more helpful resources.

Background

The Environmental Indicator Summary tool (EIST) has been developed to help users explore, visualize, and interpret the status, condition, and trends of standardized indicators of ecosystem structure and function, including soil erosion. The EIST queries data from the Landscape Data Commons , which harmonizes and aggregates core monitoring datasets and links them to wind and water erosion and remote sensing models. Data can be accessed and summarized for user-defined projects, across different land cover types, across environmental sites and regions representing differences in land potential, and across administrative units. Users can query summary tables, download full or filtered summary tables to view in Excel, explore interactive figures, and download static figures for their own use.

The EIST can be used to support research projects and decision-making processes for land managers. It can help inform conservation planning, land use plans, grazing permit renewals, shrub or invasive species treatments, seed menu development for environmental restoration and reclamation, as well as assessments of management/treatment outcomes. As new data become available, or lines of inquiry shift, the summary tables and report can be easily updated.

Data powering the EIST are from multiple sources that have been aggregated in the Landscape Data Commons. These include data collected by the Bureau of Land Management (BLM) Assessment, Inventory, and Monitoring (AIM) Program , including the Landscape Monitoring Framework (LMF) , USDA Agricultural Research Service Long-Term Agroecosystem Research (LTAR) network, National Wind Erosion Research Network (NWERN) , US Geological Survey (USGS), National Park Service (NPS), and others.

Indicators available through the EIST were derived from data collected following the Monitoring Manual for Grassland, Shrubland, and Savanna Ecosystems , the Interpreting Indicators of Rangeland Health (IIRH) assessment protocol, and models including the Aeolian EROsion (AERO) model and Rangeland Hydrology and Erosion Model (RHEM).

Using the EIST

The EIST generates tables and figures that can be queried, filtered, and downloaded to meet your research, management, and reporting needs. Data can be grouped by features such as biophysical indicators, location, or project. Spatial groupings may be beneficial if you want to compare data in, e.g., the same ecological site across multiple management units such as grazing allotments, pastures, prescribed burn areas, etc. The report you generate can be customized to select and compare indicators by both spatial and non-spatial groupings.

Step 1: Area of Interest

The EIST is organized by query group, where a query group contains at least one area of interest designed to answer a specific ecological/management question. Select your area of interest (AOI) using at least one of the following methods, to filter data geographically.

Note: There is a limit of 100,000 sites that can be queried. In the next step, you will need to validate that the number of plots is under the threshold of 100,000 before submitting your query.

You may combine any of these AOI options in one query. For example, you can select both an MLRA and a shapefile or you can select an ESD, a shapefile, and a hand-drawn polygon and they will be combined into a single area of interest.

Draw Polygon

Use the Zoom In and Zoom Out buttons on the map. Select Draw, then click on the map to start drawing a polygon. Continue clicking until the polygon is your intended size and shape. You may add additional separate polygons to the same query group. You will be able to manage overlapping polygons and exclude features in a later step.

Upload Shapefile

Select Upload to upload a shapefile from your device into the tool. For problems with the shapefile upload, see "Why is my shapefile not working?" in the Resources section.

Selectors

Use this option to select one or more projects or other land classifications. Click Selectors, then click Open Selectors List. A new window opens allowing you to choose at least one selector type: ESD, MLRA, Ecoregion Level #3, or States (US state boundaries). ESDs and MLRAs are described in more detail in Ecological Dynamics Interpretive Tool (EDIT). Ecoregions can be found on the EPA's website.

Manage Geodata

After selecting your AOI(s), the Manage Geodata table appears with a summary of your selections. Click Manage AOIs, then click the checkbox next to the AOI you wish to modify. Update the selection type, either the default Select to include sites or Exclude to subtract the sites from your query. Click Remove to delete the checked AOIs.

When your AOI selections are complete, click Next.

Select Indicators

Under the Indicators tab, click Select Indicators to open a new window. In this window's left sidebar, click the dropdown to filter the indicator options by category: Soil Attributes, Plant Attributes, Erosion Indicators, or Indicators of Rangeland Health. Validate Form

After adding time and indicator query statements, you must validate the number of plots your query contains falls under the threshold of 100,000 to proceed to the next step. If your query includes more than 100,000 plots, you will see an error message in the form. To correct this, adjust at least one selection here in Step 2 or back in Step 1 (AOI) until the number of plots is lower than 100,000. Click the Back button below to return to Step 1 to adjust your area of interest.

Note: Do not use your browser's Back or Reload buttons while using the EIST, otherwise your query selections will erase. Use the tool's Back and Next buttons at the bottom of the form to go back and forward.

Click the Validate Form button. You have successfully validated your form when the Validate Form button becomes the Next button, and a message says the form is successfully validated.

When your indicator selections are complete, and your form is validated, click Next.

Step 3: Comparison

This step is optional. Choose a time period or region to compare with your query. If you only choose a time period, the AOI will remain the same for both datasets in the comparison. If you choose an AOI, the original dataset will be compared to the data defined by the new AOI.

Click the Add Comparison button to get started. A new window opens, where you can create a comparison group with AOI or attributes to compare with your data query.

To create a comparison group, you are required to give it a name. After entering the comparison's name, use the toggle to select whether to Clone Existing with the same AOI and attribute settings in your data query, then edit for comparison. Or choose to create a New Group without AOI or attribute filters set.

Edit the comparison group's AOI and attribute filters as in previous steps for your data query.

When your comparison selections are complete, click the Validate Form button. You have successfully validated your form when the Validate Form button becomes the Next button, and a message says the form is successfully validated. Click the Next button to continue.

Terms

Term Definition
Annual biomass production The amount of organic plant matter produced in a given area over the course of a year
Land potential The inherent capacity of land to support ecological functions and services such as soil stability, vegetation cover, and agricultural or forage productivity, based on biophysical characteristics including soil texture and depth, climate, and topography. These characteristics affect potential resistance, the ability of land to maintain soil stability and avoid erosion when exposed to disturbance, and resilience, the capacity to recover soil function and vegetation cover following disturbance.
Ecological restoration and reclamation The process of assisting the recovery of severely degraded ecosystems to benefit native biota through the establishment of habitats, populations, communities, or ecosystems that are similar, but not necessarily identical to surrounding and naturally occurring ecosystems (Gerwing et al., 2021)
Ecological site A distinctive kind of land with specific soil and physical characteristics that differ from other kinds of land in its ability to produce distinctive kinds and amounts of vegetation, and in its ability to respond similarly to management actions and natural disturbances (USDA-NRCS)
Ecoregion A major ecosystem defined by distinctive geography and receiving uniform solar radiation and moisture. They are areas where ecosystems (and the type, quality, and quantity of environmental resources) are generally similar. Designed to serve as a spatial framework for the research, assessment, and monitoring of ecosystems and ecosystem components, ecoregions denote areas of similarity in the mosaic of biotic, abiotic, terrestrial, and aquatic ecosystem components with humans being considered as part of the biota. Ecoregions are identified by analyzing the patterns and composition of biotic and abiotic phenomena that affect or reflect differences in ecosystem quality and integrity (Omernik 1987) These phenomena include geology, landforms, soils, vegetation, climate, land use, wildlife, and hydrology. More details can be found on the EPA's website
Management actions Decisions and actions made under an approach to maintaining the health and productivity of an ecosystem while sustaining appropriate human uses. More details can be found in Brussard et al., 1998
Natural Disturbance Disturbances are events that cause marked changes to the impacted area. Examples of ecological disturbances include Wildfires, landslides, floods, windstorms and insect and pest outbreaks. Disturbances often come in the form of short-term or temporary changes to the landscape but can have very significant ecosystem impacts. These events often act quickly but with great impact and thereby are able to promote changes to the physical structure of the system. For example, a fire burning over a mature forest results in a different composition of species after the disturbance event. Other types of disturbances may occur over longer, persistent timeframes that also can promote changes to the biodiversity or health of the system. More details can be found on the USDA's website
Physiographic features The physical features and processes of landforms and their relation to geologic structures, including the soils, topography, land slope and aspect, and local climate, that influence the form and species composition of plant communities.
Plant community composition A description of vegetation in a fixed area by the species present and their abundance. (Jaroszynska et al.)
Plant-wildlife interactions Relationships between the plants and animals in a given ecosystem. These relationships include predation, parasitism, commensalism, or mutualism and do not require direct physical contact. Important plant-wildlife interactions are pollination, seed dispersal, and herbivory. The plants and animals have co-evolved these adaptive mechanisms to maximize the chance of survival.
State-and-Transition model These models synthesize literature and informal knowledge tied to particular ecological sites to distinguish changes in vegetation and soils that are easily reversible versus changes that are subject to thresholds beyond which reversal is costly or impossible. The models describe all known states, community phases (i.e., easily-reversible variants of states), and transitions between communities and states. Transitions contain information about mechanisms, triggers, thresholds, and indicators of threshold development. More details can be found on the Jornada website

Why is my shapefile not working?

Although a very common format for storing spatial vector information (polygons, lines, points), shapefiles can contain geometry errors or other misinformation that prevents them from functioning properly. If you are using a zipped shapefile and are not returned results, please consider the following:

  • The shapefile must be saved as a .zip file and contain the .shp, .shx, and .prj files of the shapefile. Only include one shapefile per .zip file.
  • Shapefiles may not work with all web browsers. Please use Google Chrome for best results.
  • 3D shapefiles are not supported, please convert to 2D.
  • Shapefiles can contain geometry errors. Please check and fix the geometriesof the shapefile and try again.
  • Shapefiles with complex geometries (i.e., polygons with many vertices) or many features will result in an error. Please reduce the number of features or simplify them.
  • Sliver polygons (really, really small polygons that are likely mistakes) will result in an error. Please check and fix the geometries of the shapefile.
  • Although multipart polygons are compatible with the EIST, singlepart polygons are recommended. Convert to singlepart polygons.
  • Only the first 50 features of the shapefile will be loaded; the remaining features will be discarded.
