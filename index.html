Introduction
How to use this guide:
  • The section Using the EIS Tool later in this guide has instructions for creating an ecological indicator summary (EIS).
  • Learn the science behind the EIS Tool after this introduction.
  • At the end of this guide, reference the Glossary of Terms and Acronyms and look for more helpful resources.

Background

The Ecological Indicator Summary tool (EIST) has been developed to help users explore, visualize, and interpret the status, condition, and trend of standardized indicators of ecosystem structure and function. The EIST queries data from the Landscape Data Commons , which harmonizes and aggregates core monitoring datasets and links them to wind and water erosion and remote sensing models. Data can be accessed and summarized for user-defined projects, across different land cover types, across ecological sites and regions representing differences in land potential, and across administrative units. Users can query summary tables, download full or filtered summary tables to view in Excel, explore interactive figures, and download static figures for their own use.

The EIST can be used to support research projects and decision-making processes for land managers. It can help inform conservation planning, land use plans, grazing permit renewals, shrub or invasive species treatments, seed menu development for ecological restoration and reclamation, as well as assessments of management/treatment outcomes. As new data become available, or lines of inquiry shift, the summary tables and report can be easily updated.

Data powering the EIST are from multiple sources that have been aggregated in the Landscape Data Commons. These include data collected by the Bureau of Land Management (BLM) Assessment, Inventory, and Monitoring (AIM) Program , including the Landscape Monitoring Framework (LMF) , USDA Agricultural Research Service Long-Term Agroecosystem Research (LTAR) network, National Wind Erosion Research Network (NWERN) , US Geological Survey (USGS), National Park Service (NPS), and others.

Indicators available through the EIST were derived from data collected following the Monitoring Manual for Grassland, Shrubland, and Savanna Ecosystems , the Interpreting Indicators of Rangeland Health (IIRH) assessment protocol, and models including the Aeolian Erosion (AERO) model and Rangeland Hydrology and Erosion Model (RHEM).

Using the EIS Tool

The EIST generates tables and figures that can be queried, filtered, and downloaded to meet your research, management and reporting needs. Data can be grouped by features such as biophysical indicators, location, or project. Spatial groupings may be beneficial if you want to compare data in, e.g., the same ecological site across multiple management units such as grazing allotments, pastures, prescribed burn areas, etc. The report you generate can be customized to select and compare indicators by both spatial and non-spatial groupings.

Get Started: Access the tool

To use the EIS Tool, navigate to https://www.stone-env.net/eist/.

Click "Get Started" to pull up a map and query the data. Use the tab to expand and collapse the form and map in the application.

Step 1: Area of Interest

The EIS tool is organized by query group, where a query group contains at least one area of interest designed to answer a specific ecological/management question. Select your area of interest (AOI) using at least one of the following methods, to filter data geographically.

Note: There is a limit of 10,000 sites that can be queried. In the next step, you will need to validate that the number of plots is under the threshold of 10,000 before submitting your query.

You may combine any of these AOI options in one query. For example, you can select both an MLRA and a shapefile or you can select an ESD, a shapefile, and a hand-drawn polygon and they will be combined into a single area of interest.

Draw Polygon

Use the Zoom In and Zoom Out buttons on the map. Select "Draw", then click on the map to start drawing a polygon. Continue clicking until the polygon is your intended size and shape. You may add additional separate polygons to the same query group. You will be able to manage overlapping polygons and exclude features in a later step.

Upload Shapefile

Select "Upload" to upload a shapefile from your device into the tool. For problems with the shapefile upload, see "Why is my shapefile not working?" in the Resources section.

Selectors

Use this option to select one or more projects or other land classifications. Click "Selectors", then click "Open Selectors List". A new window opens allowing you to choose at least one selector type: "ESD" (Ecological Site Description), "MLRA" (Major Land Resource Area), "Ecoregion Level #3", or "States" (US state boundaries). ESDs and MLRAs are described in more detail in Ecological Dynamics Interpretive Tool (EDIT). Ecoregions can be found on the EPA's website.

Manage Geodata

After selecting your AOI(s), the "Manage Geodata" table appears with a summary of your selections. Click "Modify AOIs", then click the checkbox next to the AOI you wish to modify. Update the selection type, either the default "Select" to include sites or "Exclude" to subtract the sites from your query. Click "Remove AOIs" to delete the checked AOIs.

Step 2: Refine by Indicator

This step is optional, however validation is required (see "Validate Form" below).

Refine your query by attribute using "Select Indicators" under the "Indicators" tab. Click the "Time" tab to set a time period filter.

Select Indicators

Under the "Indicators" tab, click "Select Indicators" to open a new window. In this window's left sidebar, click the dropdown to filter the indicator options by category: "Soil Attributes", "Plant Attributes", "Erosion Indicators", or "Indicators of Rangeland Health". Use the checkboxes next to the indicator names to include in your query. In the table of selected indicators, use the question mark icons to learn more about each indicator. Click submit to update your query.

Use the dropdown menu under "Indicator" to add one of the indicators that you selected above to your query statement. Choose an operator, then input a value to complete the statement. Add additional rows to your group to complete your query statements. You can come back and edit these statements later too, if need be. If you want to remove a query statement, you can click the yellow "X" button to the right of the row.

Indicator operator Description
= Query the data for only a specific indicator value entered in the "Value" input
!= Query the data for all EXCEPT a specific indicator value entered in the "Value" input
< Query the data for all indicator values LESS THAN a specific value entered in the "Value" input
≤ Query the data for all indicator values LESS THAN OR EQUAL TO a specific value entered in the "Value" input
> Query the data for all indicator values GREATER THAN a specific value entered in the "Value" input
≥ Query the data for all indicator values LESS THAN OR EQUAL TO a specific value entered in the "Value" input
>= & <= Query the data for all indicator values BETWEEN AND INCLUDING specific values entered in the "Minimum" and "Maximum" inputs
> & < Query the data for all Query the data for all indicator values BETWEEN specific values entered in the "Minimum" and "Maximum" inputs
Time Period

Click the "Time" tab, then select an option from the list to specify how to filter your data:

Time operator Description
Specific Date Filters for data ONLY from the date indicated
Excluding Date Filters for all data EXCEPT from the date indicated
Before Filters for all data from before and NOT including the date indicated
After Filters for all data after and NOT including the date indicated
Between Filters for all data from the period between the indicated start date and the indicated emd date, NOT including the indicated dates
Year Filters for data ONLY from the year indicated

Click "Add Time Period" to add your time filter to your data summary. The filter now appears in a table at the bottom called "Selected Time Periods". To remove a time period from your data summary, click the yellow "X" button at the end of that time period filter's row.

Validate Form

In order to proceed past Step 2, you must validate the number of plots your query contains falls under the threshold of 10,000. If your query includes more than 10,000 plots, you will see an error message in the form. To correct this, adjust at least one selection here in Step 2 or back in Step 1 (AOI) until the number of plots is lower than 10,000. Click the "Back" button to return to Step 1 to adjust your area of interest.

Note: Do not use your browser's "Back" or "Reload" buttons while using the EIS Tool, otherwise your query selections will erase. Use the tool's "Back" and "Next" buttons at the bottom of the form to go back and forward.

You have successfully validated your form when the "Validate Form" button becomes the "Next" button, and a message says the form is successfully validated.

Step 3: Comparison

This step is optional. Choose a time period or region to compare with your query. If you only choose a time period, the AOI will remain the same for both datasets in the comparison. If you choose an AOI, the original dataset will be compared to the data defined by the new AOI.

Click "Add Comparison" to get started. A new window opens, where you can create a comparison group with AOI or attributes to compare with your data query.

To create a comparison group, you are required to give it a name. After entering the comparison's name, use the toggle to select whether to "Clone Existing" with the same AOI and attribute settings in your data query, then edit for comparison. Or choose to create a "New Group" without AOI or attribute filters set.

Edit the comparison group's AOI and attribute filters as in previous steps for your data query.

Step 4: Review

Review the summary of your query details in the table. If you did not add a comparison group, you should see only one group listed, named "Default", which is your data summary query.

Use the Back button to edit your selections. Press Submit Query to run the query and view your results.

Note: Do not use your browser's "Back" or "Reload" buttons while using the EIS Tool, otherwise your query selections will erase. Use the tool's "Back" and "Next" buttons at the bottom of the form to go back and forward.

Once you have reviewed your query, click "Submit Query" to run the query. You may need to wait a moment for the data to load. If there are errors, follow the instructions provided to fix the error.

Results

Review your query data summary, graph indicators, and generate reports.

Click the button "Select Results from Query Group" and select the group for which you want to view results. If you created a comparison query, click "Select Groups to Compare" to select the query results to compare using the checkboxes.

After selecting the query group(s) results to view, the graph appears in the sidebar. Use the "Box"-"Histogram" toggle to switch between a box plot and histogram format.

Create New Graph

Click "Create New Graph" button in the footer at the bottom of the results. A new window called "Select Fields to Graph" opens.

Like the "Select Indicators" window you used to create your data summary, use this window's left sidebar to filter the indicator options by category: "Soil Attributes", "Plant Attributes", "Erosion Indicators", or "Indicators of Rangeland Health". Use the checkboxes next to the indicator names to include in your graph.

Click the "Delete All Graphs" button to delete all graphs you created.

Note: You cannot undo "Delete All Graphs".

Your data summary will still be available to create new graphs.

Export Results

Click the "Export Results" button in the sidebar footer. A menu of options appears:

  • Report: Click this option to generate a PDF report of your data summary, including customized content.
  • Graphs (.zip): Click to export all graphs as a zipped folder of image files.
  • Shapefile: Click to export your data summary results as a shapefile.

Terms
Term Definition
Annual biomass production The amount of organic plant matter produced in a given area over the course of a year
Ecological potential The greatest potential fractional cover (bare ground, herbaceous, litter, shrub, and sagebrush) represented in the least disturbed areas and most productive years of the record for a given plot of land. It is a measurement of how much biomass could grow in a region given perfect conditions
Ecological restoration and reclamation The process of assisting the recovery of severely degraded ecosystems to benefit native biota through the establishment of habitats, populations, communities, or ecosystems that are similar, but not necessarily identical to surrounding and naturally occurring ecosystems (Gerwing et al., 2021)
Ecological site A distinctive kind of land with specific soil and physical characteristics that differ from other kinds of land in its ability to produce distinctive kinds and amounts of vegetation, and in its ability to respond similarly to management actions and natural disturbances (USDA-NRCS)
Ecoregion A major ecosystem defined by distinctive geography and receiving uniform solar radiation and moisture. They are areas where ecosystems (and the type, quality, and quantity of environmental resources) are generally similar. Designed to serve as a spatial framework for the research, assessment, and monitoring of ecosystems and ecosystem components, ecoregions denote areas of similarity in the mosaic of biotic, abiotic, terrestrial, and aquatic ecosystem components with humans being considered as part of the biota. Ecoregions are identified by analyzing the patterns and composition of biotic and abiotic phenomena that affect or reflect differences in ecosystem quality and integrity (Omernik 1987) These phenomena include geology, landforms, soils, vegetation, climate, land use, wildlife, and hydrology. More details can be found on the EPA's website
Management actions Decisions and actions made under an approach to maintaining the health and productivity of an ecosystem while sustaining appropriate human uses. More details can be found in Brussard et al., 1998
Natural Disturbance Disturbances are events, like tornados, wildfires or floods that cause marked changes to the impacted area. Examples of ecological disturbances include fires, landslides, flooding, windstorms and insect and pest outbreaks. Disturbances often come in the form of short-term or temporary changes to the landscape but can have very significant ecosystem impacts. These events often act quickly but with great impact and thereby are able to promote changes to the physical structure of the system. For example, a fire burning over a mature forest results in a different composition of species after the disturbance event. Other types of disturbances may occur over longer, persistent timeframes that also can promote changes to the biodiversity or health of the system. More details can be found on the USDA's website
Physiographic features The physical features and processes of landforms and their relation to geologic structures, including the soils, topography, land slope and aspect, and local climate, that influence the form and species composition of plant communities.
Plant community composition A description of vegetation in a fixed area by the species present and their abundance. (Jaroszynska et al.)
Plant-wildlife interactions Relationships between the plants and animals in a given ecosystem. These relationships include predation, parasitism, commensalism, or mutualism and do not require direct physical contact. Important plant-wildlife interactions are pollination, seed dispersal, and herbivory. The plants and animals have co-evolved these adaptive mechanisms to maximize the chance of survival.
State-and-Transition model These models synthesize literature and informal knowledge tied to particular ecological sites to distinguish changes in vegetation and soils that are easily reversible versus changes that are subject to thresholds beyond which reversal is costly or impossible. The models describe all possible states, community phases (i.e., easily-reversible variants of states), and transitions between communities and states. Transitions contain information about mechanisms, triggers, thresholds, and indicators of threshold development. More details can be found on the Jornada website

Why is my shapefile not working?

Although a very common format for storing spatial vector information (polygons, lines, points), shapefiles can contain geometry errors or other misinformation that prevents them from functioning properly. If you are using a zipped shapefile and are not returned results, please consider the following:

  • The shapefile must be saved as a .zip file and contain the .shp, .shx, and .prj files of the shapefile. Only include one shapefile per .zip file.
  • Shapefiles may not work with all web browsers. Please use Google Chrome for best results.
  • 3D shapefiles are not supported, please convert to 2D.
  • Shapefiles can contain geometry errors. Please check and fix the geometriesof the shapefile and try again.
  • Shapefiles with complex geometries (i.e., polygons with many vertices) or many features will result in an error. Please reduce the number of features or simplify them.
  • Sliver polygons (really, really small polygons that are likely mistakes) will result in an error. Please check and fix the geometries of the shapefile.
  • Although multipart polygons are compatible with the EIST, singlepart polygons are recommended. Convert to singlepart polygons.
  • Only the first 50 features of the shapefile will be loaded; the remaining features will be discarded.
